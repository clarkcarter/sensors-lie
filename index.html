What happens when a robot's sensors lie?

I assumed this was a solved problem. Robots have sensors. Sometimes sensors break. Surely there's a standard way to figure out which one. I went looking for it about a year ago, didn't find it, and accidentally spent the next twelve months building one.

Let me back up.

I was reading about warehouse robots. This is just the kind of weird nerd stuff I read before bed. Don't judge me. The little flat ones that scoot around fulfilment centres carrying shelves. Turns out they have a bunch of sensors: encoders on the wheels, an IMU for orientation, LIDAR for obstacle avoidance, sometimes GPS. These sensors are how the robot understands where it is and what's around it. They are, in a very literal sense, the robot's entire model of reality.

And sometimes they break. Not in a dramatic, Hollywood way. Nobody's encoder has ever exploded. What actually happens is way more annoying. The IMU starts drifting a little high. Or the GPS locks onto a signal bouncing off a metal shelf and briefly decides the robot teleported three metres to the left. Thanks, physics.

Now the robot is getting conflicting information. Some sensors are telling the truth. At least one is lying. And the robot has no idea which one.

This matters because robots move. A warehouse AGV covers 20 metres in 10 seconds. An autonomous car on a highway covers 330. That's a lot of ground to travel while trusting a sensor that's lying to you.

There's an important distinction here that took me a while to appreciate. Detecting that something is wrong is relatively easy. You look at your sensors and notice they disagree. Fine. Isolating which specific sensor is the liar is a completely different problem. You've got four sensors, three saying one thing and one saying another. Except sometimes the faulty one is subtle enough that it's not obvious which group is wrong. Or two sensors are drifting in different ways. Or the one your fusion algorithm trusts most is the one that's broken.

Detection is largely a solved problem. Isolation is not. That's the rabbit hole.

* * *

The existing solutions all have the same basic issue: they don't scale.

In aerospace they solve this by installing three copies of every sensor and voting. If one disagrees with the other two, it's the liar. Elegant. Also, you just tripled your sensor cost, weight, and wiring. And it only works with identical sensors. A self-driving car doesn't have three LIDARs, three radars, and three cameras. It has one of each because they measure different things. You can't vote across different sensor types.

In control theory you build mathematical models for every possible failure mode and run them all in parallel. Works if you can enumerate every way a sensor might fail, which in practice means serious engineering per configuration. Change a sensor, redo the models.

You can throw machine learning at it. Of course you can. You can throw machine learning at anything. You'll need labelled examples of every fault type on every sensor, it's a black box that no safety certifier will touch, and you retrain every time something changes. But hey, at least you can put AI on your pitch deck.

Three approaches. Same problem. They all get harder as you add more sensors. And the trend in robotics is more sensors, not fewer.

* * *

So here's where I fell in. I should be upfront: I am not a roboticist. I don't have a PhD in control theory. I don't even work in this industry. I'm a generalist who got nerd-sniped and apparently has no self-control.

The mathematical ideas behind what I ended up building aren't new. There's research on using graph theory to reason about sensor consistency that predates my work by years. But as far as I could tell, nobody had taken those ideas and turned them into a working, certifiable system. The theory existed. The product didn't.

So I just... followed my interest and built one. Twelve months of "I'll just try one more thing." I did *jazz hands* math stuff. I told my mates Amazon was going to buy it for $100 million. They nodded politely. Amazon did not buy it for $100 million.

I called it HOLMES, because it identifies the guilty party from a lineup of suspects. Yes I'm aware the name is a bit much. I'm keeping it.

The core idea is this. Sensors measuring the same physical scene create natural consistency relationships. Your GPS and your encoder both measure position, so they should agree within their noise levels. Your IMU and LIDAR both measure motion, so their derivatives should be consistent. You can represent all these relationships as a graph and then ask a beautifully simple question: which node, if you removed it, would make everything else agree?

I will not pretend I got here in a straight line. My first attempt was embarrassingly naive and basically didn't work at all. I'd ask Claude Code if the approach was sound and it would tell me it was brilliant, revolutionary even. Then I'd actually test it and nothing worked. Then I'd go back and ask what went wrong and it would say "ah yes, I see the issue" like it hadn't just told me it was perfect. Rinse, repeat, for months.

But the graph framing felt right, and the more I dug into the maths (with significantly less trust in my AI cheerleader) the more I realised the question above has a precise answer. One that doesn't need training data or pre-built models. Just each sensor's noise spec (on the datasheet) and the physical relationships between them.

I built a cascade of statistical tests on top of this, each targeting a different kind of fault: dropout, drift, gain shift, stuck readings, noise increase, delay. Then I added a cross-validation layer using an independent method. When the two approaches disagree, the system doesn't guess. It just goes quiet.

That "going quiet" part is the thing I'm most proud of. A wrong answer is actively dangerous. Silence is always safe. So I designed the entire system around the principle that not knowing is fine. Confidently blaming the wrong sensor is not. 

Sensors ──→ [Your Fault Detector] ──→ [HOLMES] ──→  "it's sensor 3"
                    │                     │
                "anomaly!"                ├──→  "sensors 2 and 5"
                                          │
                                          └──→  "not sure, abstaining"
* * *

I expected the results to be OK. Maybe good enough to be interesting. I was not prepared for what actually happened.

I built a benchmark modelling four real configurations: a 4-sensor warehouse AGV, a 5-sensor UAV, a 6-sensor autonomous vehicle, and an 8-sensor underground mining rig. Every combination of fault type, faulty sensor, and noise profile. Five random seeds. Fully deterministic. Same code, same seed, same result every time.

465,750 test scenarios. 24 wrong answers. I checked the numbers three times.

That's a wrong isolation rate of 0.005%. The rest of the time it either correctly identified the faulty sensor or said "not sure" and abstained.

I also stress tested it at up to 20 times the rated sensor noise. Mining sites, rough seas, that kind of thing. At extreme noise levels the system just gets more cautious. It abstains more. But it almost never gets it wrong.

The whole thing runs at 10 Hz on commodity hardware. No GPU, no training step, no parameter tuning. Every threshold is derived from sensor physics. It's designed for IEC 61508 SIL 3 certification, which is the safety standard that governs whether you're allowed to put this kind of thing on an actual robot.

* * *

Naturally, the next step was to ask my AI how much this could be worth and start window shopping electric Lamborghinis. I ran the numbers on licensing deals. I drafted pitch emails. I imagined the TechCrunch headline. It turns out (and I cannot stress how shocking this was to me) that having a good thing on your laptop and getting anyone to actually pay money for it are two completely unrelated skills. Nobody was interested. Not even a little bit.

So here I am, writing this up, because maybe the internet can help. The numbers are hard to argue with, and I've argued with them plenty.

What I don't have is real sensor data from real robots. Everything so far is simulated (realistic noise profiles, realistic fault modes, but still synthetic). If you work on autonomous systems and have sensor logs collecting dust on a hard drive somewhere, I would love to throw HOLMES at them and see what happens.
